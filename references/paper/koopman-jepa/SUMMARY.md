# Koopman-JEPA — JEPA's predictive loss implicitly drives its encoder to learn the eigenvalue-1 invariant subspace of the data's Koopman operator, explaining why JEPAs spontaneously cluster time series by dynamical regime

**Authors:** Pablo Ruiz-Morales, Dries Vanoost, Davy Pissoort, Mathias Verbeke (KU Leuven / Flanders Make) **Venue/Year:** AAAI 2026 (pre-print, accepted) **arXiv:** 2511.09783 (v2, 23 Jan 2026) **Repo:** none stated (project website https://dn-isense.eu/)

## TL;DR
A purely theoretical paper (one synthetic validation) explaining a known empirical curiosity: when applied to time series, JEPAs spontaneously cluster latent embeddings by the data's underlying *dynamical regime*, where a same-capacity reconstruction autoencoder does not. The authors formalize this with **Koopman operator theory**. They prove that an idealized JEPA loss is globally minimized when the encoder's components span the *invariant subspace* V of the system's Δ-step Koopman operator (the eigenspace for eigenvalue 1), and that this subspace is exactly the span of the regime *indicator functions* χ_i. The optimal predictor on this subspace is the **identity**. Crucially the optimal solution is only unique up to an invertible linear change of basis (the loss is invariant to it); initializing the linear predictor M near identity is the **inductive bias** that selects the interpretable, disentangled basis from the entangled equivalence class. Validated on a synthetic 18-regime time-series dataset: JEPA reaches 65.5% cluster purity vs 38.8% for a matched AE, and an identity-initialized linear predictor M converges to near-identity (2.34% relative Frobenius error from I, eigenvalue spectrum dominated by 18 eigenvalues near 1.0).

## Problem & motivation
SSL methods (SimCLR, BYOL, SimSiam, JEPA) learn rich representations but the *mechanism* by which they discover structure is opaque. A specific, reproducible-but-unexplained phenomenon motivates the paper: JEPAs applied to time series produce latent embeddings that **spontaneously cluster by unannotated dynamical regime**, and this is *not* a generic encoder-capacity effect, because a reconstruction-based autoencoder with the identical encoder architecture fails to separate the same regimes. The central question: *why does JEPA's predictive objective, and not reconstruction, lead to regime-aware clustering?* The paper distinguishes JEPA's *implicit* mechanism from explicit dynamical/disentanglement methods (Koopman autoencoders, VAMPnets, time-lagged AEs, β-VAE) that bake the target into a specialized objective, and from contrastive time-series SSL (TS2Vec) that learns similarity from temporal proximity. The hypothesis: JEPA's purely *predictive* latent objective is what drives it to learn functions invariant under the system's evolution within each regime.

## Method
The contribution is a theory (the Koopman-operator analysis), not a new architecture.

### Setup and idealized loss
Time-series windows x_t = (s_t,...,s_{t+n-1}) ∈ X are drawn from a stationary process with invariant measure µ. JEPA has an online encoder f_θ: X→R^k, an online predictor g_φ: R^k→R^k, and an EMA target encoder f_EMA (decay α≈0.996–0.999). The training loss is the standard predictive MSE
L(θ,φ) = E_{x_t~µ}[‖g_φ(f_θ(x_t)) − f_EMA(x_{t+Δ})‖²₂] (Eq. 1).
**Key idealization:** assuming f_EMA closely tracks f_θ (‖θ−θ_EMA‖ small), the loss reduces to L(f,g) ≈ E[‖g(f(x_t)) − f(x_{t+Δ})‖²₂] (Eq. 2), the basis for all theory.

### Koopman operator
**Definition 2.1 (Δ-step Koopman operator).** K ≡ K(Δ): L²(X,µ)→L²(X,µ) maps an observable ψ to its conditional expectation Δ steps ahead: (Kψ)(x) = E[ψ(x_{t+Δ}) | x_t = x] (Eq. 3). K is *linear* regardless of the nonlinearity of the underlying system, and is a contraction on L²(X,µ). Eigenfunctions ψ_j satisfy Kψ_j = ν_j ψ_j; those with **ν_j = 1 are invariant in expectation** under the Δ-step dynamics. The encoder f_θ(x) is read as a vector of observables.

### Regimes and the invariant subspace
**Assumption 3.1 (Finite Mixture of Ergodic Regimes).** µ = Σ_{i=1}^r α_i µ_i, a finite convex mixture of r distinct ergodic measures supported on essentially-disjoint sets X_i, with **dynamical immiscibility**: a trajectory starting in X_i stays in X_i forever. This lets one define regime indicators χ_i(x) := 1_{X_i}(x), spanning the r-dim subspace V = span{χ_1,...,χ_r}.

**Lemma 3.2 (Properties of Regime Indicators and V).** Under Assumption 3.1: (a) each χ_i is an eigenfunction of K with eigenvalue 1 (Kχ_i = χ_i); (b) each χ_i is pathwise invariant over Δ steps (χ_i(x_{t+Δ}) = χ_i(x_t)); (c) any ψ ∈ V satisfies both Kψ = ψ and ψ(x_{t+Δ}) = ψ(x_t); (d) **V is precisely the eigenspace of K for eigenvalue 1, and dim V = r** (from ergodic decomposition theory, citing Petersen 1983; Eisner et al. 2015 — the eigenvalue-1 eigenspace dimension equals the number of ergodic components). The paper stresses 3.2(c): functions in V are not merely invariant in expectation but *pathwise perfectly predictable* (f(x_{t+Δ}) = f(x_t)), making them prime candidates for what JEPA learns.

### Loss decomposition (Eq. 5, proved in App. A)
With a **linear predictor** g(z) = Mz (Assumption 3.3) and f(x) = ⃗ψ(x), the loss splits into two non-negative terms (the cross-term vanishes exactly because E[⃗ψ(x_{t+Δ})|x_t] = (K⃗ψ)(x_t) by definition of K):
L(f,M) = E_x[‖M⃗ψ(x) − (K⃗ψ)(x)‖²₂] (Term 1: **Mean Prediction Error** — can the linear predictor match the conditional expectation) + E_x[‖(K⃗ψ)(x) − ⃗ψ(x_{t+Δ})‖²₂] (Term 2: **Inherent Stochasticity Error** — how far observables deviate from their conditional expectation along real trajectories). L = 0 iff both terms are zero.

### Main theorem
**Theorem 3.4 (JEPA Learns Regime Indicators).** Under Assumptions 3.1, 3.3 and encoder capacity k ≥ r, the loss (5) achieves its global minimum (0) iff (a) (Kf_j)(x) = f_j(x_{t+Δ}) µ-a.e. for each component j (Term 2 zero), and (b) Mf(x) = (Kf)(x) µ-a.e. (Term 1 zero). These are simultaneously satisfied if the encoder components f_j ∈ V and M acts as the identity on the subspace of R^k spanned by f(X). Concretely, for f*(x) = (χ_1(x),...,χ_r(x), 0_{k-r})^T, the loss is minimized by any M* whose action on the span of the non-zero components is identity (the App. A proof shows M*'s top-left r×r block = I_r, the lower-left block = 0; the blocks multiplying the zero components are arbitrary). The paper notes the intuition **extends to nonlinear predictors**: if z_t = f(x_t) ∈ V then z_{t+Δ} = z_t a.s., so E[z_{t+Δ}|z_t] = z_t and an optimal nonlinear predictor likewise approximates an identity map g_φ(z_t) ≈ z_t — functions in V are "the epitome of such simplicity."

### Why clustering, and the disentanglement insight
If f(x) = A⃗χ(x) for a rank-r k×r matrix A, then a window in regime X_i has ⃗χ(x) = e_i, so f(x) = A e_i = the i-th column of A. **All windows from regime X_i map to the same latent point**, yielding r distinct clusters. But the loss is invariant to any invertible linear change of basis of the latent space, so this optimal subspace V is recovered only *up to an entangled basis*; the canonical, interpretable (axis-aligned) solution is selected by the **inductive bias of initializing the predictor M near identity** — this is the paper's "predictor's role in representation disentanglement."

## Key results
Synthetic dataset constructed to satisfy Assumption 3.1 by design: **r = 18 immiscible dynamical regimes** (sinusoids of varied freq/amp/harmonics, square/sawtooth waves, AR/MA/ARMA stochastic processes, linear trends, sparse pulses, sine+trend, high-noise sine), 10,000 sequences each = 180,000 context-target pairs, deterministic 70/20/10 split, per-sequence standardized; deterministic signals noise-free (only ARMA carry intrinsic process noise). Encoder = 4-layer 1D-CNN + linear head, latent k = 32 (≥ r = 18). Predictor is either a 2-hidden-layer MLP, or a linear M ∈ R^{32×32} (no bias), initialized either as identity or randomly. Context window n_c = 768, horizon Δ = 256, EMA α = 0.996.

- **Clustering (Fig. 3, K-Means K=18 purity):** JEPA (with MLP predictor) reaches **65.48% mean cluster purity**; a matched autoencoder reaches only **38.81%**. Demonstrates the predictive *objective*, not encoder capacity, drives regime separation.
- **Learned linear predictor M (identity-initialized):** converges to a near-perfect identity — relative Frobenius error ‖M−I_k‖_F/‖M‖_F = **2.34%**; skew-symmetric relative norm ‖M−Mᵀ‖_F/‖M‖_F = **2.06%**; eigenvalue spectrum (Fig. 4) **dominated by r=18 eigenvalues near 1.0** with the rest attenuated.
- **M's action on regime centroids (Fig. 5):** for the 18 K-Means centroids c_i, the relative error ‖Mc_i − c_i‖₂/‖c_i‖₂ averages just **0.80%**, confirming M ≈ identity on V as predicted.
- **Non-uniqueness control:** a randomly-initialized M reaches the same low loss and clusters visibly, but yields a **dense, non-identity** transform — confirming the change-of-basis invariance, and that identity-init is what selects the interpretable basis.

## Relevance to the EB-JEPA hackathon
This is a **theory paper** that informs how we reason about JEPA representations, collapse, and world-model dynamics; it is cross-cutting rather than track-specific.
- **Collapse understanding / regularizer design:** it frames the *good* attractor of the JEPA loss as "encoder spans the eigenvalue-1 invariant subspace; predictor → identity on it." This is the opposite of trivial collapse, and gives a principled lens: a variance/covariance regularizer (VICReg/SIGReg) should aim to keep the encoder spanning the full r-dim invariant subspace (rank r) rather than collapsing to a strict subspace, while the predictor-near-identity bias picks a disentangled basis. The change-of-basis invariance argument is directly relevant to *why* covariance/whitening regularizers and predictor design matter for disentanglement.
- **World-model dynamics:** the conditional-expectation reading of the predictor, g_φ(z_t) ≈ E[z_{t+Δ}|z_t], and the "predictable = invariant" principle are exactly the world-modeling lens used in EB-JEPA's action-conditioned/video JEPAs — it argues the latent should make future representations *simply* (here, identically) predictable, which is the world-model objective.
- **Diagnostic tools:** the M-matrix battery (Frobenius distance from I, symmetry, eigenvalue spectrum near 1.0, action on cluster centroids) is a cheap, reusable probe to check whether *any* JEPA's learned predictor is behaving as a near-identity / preserving the invariant subspace.
- **Predictor inductive bias:** the empirical finding that *identity-initializing the predictor* selects an interpretable basis with no loss penalty is a concrete, near-zero-cost trick worth trying on EB-JEPA predictors.
- **Closest track:** time-series / signal-modality JEPA (overlaps the S-JEPA EEG track — both are 1D multi-channel temporal JEPAs), but the theory generalizes to image/video JEPAs treated as dynamical systems.

## Caveats & open threads
- **Synthetic-only validation, idealized data:** the single experiment uses synthetic data built to satisfy Assumption 3.1 *exactly* (immiscible, ergodic, essentially noise-free). The authors flag that real time series have gradual transitions, hierarchical structure, and noise that violate immiscibility — none tested.
- **Two theoretical idealizations:** (i) the EMA target is approximated by f_EMA ≈ f_θ, collapsing the actual EMA dynamics; the authors concede a "deeper analysis of the EMA's stabilizing role" is missing. (ii) The clean global-minimum proof assumes a *linear* predictor (Assumption 3.3); the nonlinear case is argued only by intuition.
- **"Only-if" of the theorem is conditional:** the converse direction of Theorem 3.4 (that a zero-loss solution forces f_j ∈ V) is proved only *under the added assumption* that M acts as identity on f(X) (the "simplest predictor" case) — it is not unconditional.
- **Modest purity:** 65.48% cluster purity is well short of perfect even on noise-free synthetic data, so the "clean" theoretical optimum is only approximately realized in practice.
- **Open directions (authors):** validate on real-world data; compare JEPA's implicit discovery to explicit Koopman identification; explore whether the architecture can learn *other* Koopman eigenfunctions (oscillatory / slow-decay modes, |ν|≠1) beyond the eigenvalue-1 invariants; design new SSL objectives that explicitly leverage Koopman theory for regime identification and anomaly detection (anomalies = deviations from learned regime clusters).
