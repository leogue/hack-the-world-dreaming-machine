# Graph-JEPA — The first JEPA for the graph domain: predict latent embeddings of masked subgraphs (patches) from one context subgraph, with a hyperbolic-angle prediction target that bakes hierarchy into the latent space.

**Authors:** Geri Skenderi (Bocconi University), Hang Li (Michigan State), Jiliang Tang (Michigan State), Marco Cristani (University of Verona) **Venue/Year:** TMLR (arXiv v3, 18 Jan 2025; original Sep 2023) **arXiv:** 2309.16014 **Repo:** https://github.com/geriskenderi/graph-jepa

## TL;DR
Graph-JEPA ports the I-JEPA masked-latent-prediction recipe to graph-level representation learning. An input graph is partitioned into `p` non-overlapping subgraphs ("patches") via METIS, each expanded by a 1-hop neighborhood, then encoded by a GNN (GINE) into per-patch vectors. One random patch is the **context** and `m` others are **targets**. A predictor regresses each target patch's latent, conditioned on that patch's positional embedding (RWSE). The twist vs vanilla JEPA: instead of predicting the target's raw latent in Euclidean space, the target latent is mapped to a scalar **hyperbolic angle** `α = (1/N)Σ Zy` and then to **2D unit-hyperbola coordinates** `[cosh(α), sinh(α)]`; the predictor outputs `ψ̂ ∈ R²` and the loss is **smooth L1** on those 2D coords. This makes the prediction a cheap low-D regression while implicitly endowing representations with hierarchy. Collapse is prevented by the standard self-distillation tricks: **EMA target encoder + stop-gradient**, plus a deliberately **simple (linear-ish MLP) predictor**. Pretrained per-fold on small TUD datasets, it sets SOTA as a frozen backbone on 5 of 8 benchmarks, runs "up to 2.5x faster than GraphMAE and 8x faster than MVGRL," and a linear probe on its features distinguishes 98.77% of 1-WL-indistinguishable graph pairs (vs ~50% for GCN/GINE).

## Problem & motivation
Graph SSL splits into **contrastive** methods (InfoGraph, GraphCL, MVGRL, AD-GCL), which "require a lot of memory due to data augmentation and negative samples" and need carefully designed graph augmentations, and **generative** methods (GAE/VGAE, GraphMAE, S2GAE), which "display strong overfitting tendencies" and whose features "are not always useful for downstream tasks" because they must estimate a complex data distribution, "even more problematic for graphs because they live in a non-Euclidean and inhomogenous data space." JEPAs (LeCun 2022; I-JEPA) bridge the two: they predict in latent space (no negatives, no augmentation, no pixel/structure reconstruction). Graph-JEPA is "the first architecture implementing the modern JEPA principles for the graph domain." The named design challenges: (i) context/target extraction on irregular graphs, (ii) a latent prediction task suited to graph-level concepts, (iii) learning expressive representations. The authors further "conjecture that the typical latent reconstruction objective used in current JEPA formulations is not enough" because "the nature of graph-level concepts is often assumed to be hierarchical (Ying et al., 2018)" — motivating the hyperbolic target.

## Method
Four-stage pipeline: (i) Spatial Partitioning; (ii) Subgraph Embedding; (iii) Context/Target Encoding; (iv) Latent Target Prediction.

### (i) Spatial partitioning into subgraph patches
The graph `G=(V,E)` is split into `p` disjoint subsets `{V₁..Vₚ}`, `Vᵢ∩Vⱼ=∅`, `∪Vᵢ=V`, via **METIS** (a multilevel clustering algorithm that minimizes inter-cluster edge cuts), so each patch carries locality/community structure — and METIS's "multilevel nature" relates to the "hierarchical nature of graph-level concepts." Because disjoint patches can lose edges or be edgeless, a **1-hop neighborhood expansion** connects each patch's nodes to their original-graph neighbors, enabling cross-region signal flow. Ablation: random patches work nearly as well as METIS (Table 6a), but METIS is "advocated... as a safer option in terms of generalizability."

### (ii) Subgraph embedding + positional encoding
A **GINE** GNN (GIN with edge-feature support) encodes nodes; node embeddings are **mean-pooled** per patch to `{h₁..hₚ}, h∈R^d`. Positional info per patch is the **Random Walk Structural Embedding (RWSE)**: `Pᵥ = (M_ii, M²_ii, ..., M^k_ii)` where `M = D⁻¹A` is the random-walk transition matrix; the patch's RWSE is the elementwise `max` over its nodes (`Pₗ = max_{v∈Vₗ} Pᵥ`), so it is anchored on the highest-degree node and gives a "global context" injective signal. Ablation: node-level (global) RWSE consistently beats relative patch-level RWSE (Table 6b; e.g. ZINC 0.434 vs 0.505).

### (iii) Context/target encoding (I-JEPA-style)
Per training step, choose **one** random context patch `x` and `m` target patches `Y={y₁..yₘ}`. Encoders are **pre-norm Transformer encoder blocks**: the context encoder `Ec` has no self-attention, the target encoder `Et` uses Hadamard self-attention. `zx = Ec(x) ∈ R^d`, `Zy = Et(Y) ∈ R^{m×d}`. Using a single context "guarantees that we can work with small and large graphs" and "implicitly regularizes the model in learning long-range dependencies by predicting targets that are far... from the context."

### (iv) Latent target prediction (the hyperbolic twist) + loss
Rather than predict `Zy` directly in `R^d`, each target patch latent is collapsed to a scalar hyperbolic angle and lifted to 2D unit-hyperbola coords (Eq. 4):
`ψy_l = [cosh(αy_l), sinh(αy_l)]`, `αy_l = (1/N) Σ_{n=1}^{d} Zy_l(n)`.
The predictor (Eq. 5) is a 2-layer MLP conditioned on the **sum of context embedding and the target's positional encoding**:
`ψ̂y_l = W₂(σ(W₁(zx + Pₗ) + b₁)) + b₂`, with `ψ̂y_l ∈ R²`.
Loss (Eq. 6) is the **smooth L1 (Huber)** distance between `ψy_l` and `ψ̂y_l`, "less sensitive to outliers compared to the typical L2 loss." Intuition: in high dimensions `α` concentrates around one value (the "base level of the hierarchy") while encoders can produce "largely deviant latent codes that lead to higher hierarchy levels." This estimates a restricted section of the hyperboloid model where hyperbolas are geodesics, getting hyperbolic-style hierarchy without the dimensionality/stability tradeoffs of Poincaré-ball embeddings.

### Anti-collapse
"JEPAs are based on a self-distillation procedure. Therefore, they are by definition susceptible to representation collapse." Two tricks: (i) **stop-gradient on the target encoder + EMA momentum update** from the context encoder weights — formalized via an OLS argument: with a linear predictor solving `argmin_W ‖XW − Y‖²`, if the target encoder shared weights/optimizer with the context encoder, "the easiest solution... would be learning a representation that is orthogonal to itself, i.e., the 0 vector, leading to representation collapse"; (ii) a **deliberately simple/less-expressive predictor**, because "if the predictor network is sufficiently powerful, it can predict the target representations even without a well-learned context representation," yielding uninformative features. There is **no VICReg/SIGReg-style explicit variance-covariance regularizer** — collapse prevention is purely the BYOL/I-JEPA EMA+stop-grad+weak-predictor asymmetry.

### Downstream protocol
All `p` subgraphs are passed through the trained **target encoder** and **mean-pooled** into one graph vector `zG ∈ R^d`, then a **linear** model with L2 reg is fit (Logistic Regression for classification, Ridge for ZINC regression). Pretraining is run **per fold on the training split only** (never touching the test partition), unlike standard protocols that pretrain on the full dataset.

## Key results
**Datasets / setup.** Seven TUD graph-classification datasets (PROTEINS, MUTAG, DD, REDDIT-BINARY, REDDIT-MULTI-5K, IMDB-BINARY, IMDB-MULTI), 10-fold CV over 5 seeds; ZINC for regression (MSE over 10 seeds). Hyperparams (Table 8): embedding size **512** everywhere, **4** encoder blocks, **2–3** GNN layers, **32 or 128** subgraphs, 1 context with **2–4** targets, RWSE size 15–40. Trained with Adam on RTX 3090s; PyTorch + PyTorch-Geometric.

**Downstream SOTA (Table 1).** Graph-JEPA "set[s] the state-of-the-art as a pretrained backbone on five different datasets and com[es] second on one (out of eight total)" and "performs better than a supervised GIN on all classification datasets." Selected numbers — MUTAG **91.25 ± 5.75** (best), DD **78.64 ± 2.35** (best), REDDIT-B **91.99 ± 1.59** (best), REDDIT-M5 **56.73 ± 1.96** (best), PROTEINS 75.68 ± 3.78 (2nd, behind S2GAE 76.37), IMDB-B 73.68 ± 3.24, IMDB-M 50.69 ± 2.91, ZINC **0.434 ± 0.014** (best SSL; supervised F-GIN 0.254). **Variance is high** (note the ±3–6 on several datasets), attributed to (a) random partitioning sometimes picking trivial context patches, (b) collapse risk with small similar patches + positional embeddings, (c) deliberately untuned hyperparameters, (d) the strict train-split-only pretraining.

**Expressivity vs 1-WL (Table 2).** On the synthetic EXP dataset (600 pairs of non-isomorphic graphs indistinguishable by 1-WL), a **linear probe on frozen Graph-JEPA features hits 98.77 ± 0.99** vs ~50% for supervised GCN (51.90), GatedGCN (51.73), GINE (50.69), GraphTransformer (52.35) — nearly matching the fully-supervised Graph-MLP-Mixer (100.00).

**Efficiency (Table 4).** "up to 2.5x faster than Graph-MAE... and 8x faster than MVGRL." On IMDB: Graph-JEPA **56 s** (19.2M params) vs GraphMAE ~96 s vs MVGRL ~7 min; on REDDIT-M5: **~18 min** vs GraphMAE ~46 min vs MVGRL OOM. (Graph-JEPA has ~8x more params, 19.2M, but trains faster.)

**Latent-space ablation (Table 3).** Hyperbolic-angle target beats Euclidean and Poincaré objectives overall: ZINC 0.434 (ours) vs 0.471 (Euclidean) vs 0.605 (Poincaré) vs 0.952/0.912 (low-dim variants); on IMDB-B the Poincaré objective gives **NaN** in high dim (instability). 3D t-SNE (Fig. 3) shows visibly different curvature under the Graph-JEPA objective.

**Other ablations.** MLP encoders (no attention) still work but Transformer encoders give better/lower-variance results (Table 5). METIS ≈ random partitioning (Table 6a). Node RWSE > relative patch RWSE (Table 6b).

**Long-range (LRGB, appendix Table 9).** On Peptides-struct (regression) Graph-JEPA is competitive with supervised baselines (Test MAE **0.305 ± 0.002**, Train R² 0.731 vs SAN+RWSE 0.2545 MAE), but on Peptides-func (multilabel classification) it **collapses to 0.263 ± 0.003 AP vs 0.644 for SAN+RWSE** — "excels at capturing smooth global features... but fails to capture localized, specific interactions... due to the nature of the subgraph self-predictive task." Ablations there (†=no PE, ‡=no PE+Euclidean target) confirm both the positional embedding and the hyperbolic objective help.

## Relevance to the EB-JEPA hackathon
Graphs are **not an explicit track** in the hackathon guide, but Graph-JEPA is a clean template for a **Group A "carry the JEPA recipe to a new modality"** project; its closest listed sibling is **Track 6 (3D point clouds / LiDAR)** — both target an **unordered, irregular, non-grid modality** where "the recipe transfers to an unordered, irregular modality" is exactly the research question. It also rhymes with the guide's **"new modality with no spatial grid"** advice (graphs have no `[H,W]` grid; a patch is a feature vector per subgraph).

**Mapping onto an eb_jepa example.** Graph-JEPA is **spatial masked-patch latent prediction within a single graph**, NOT temporal next-step prediction. So it maps to **`examples/image_jepa`** (the masked/two-input invariance setting), not `video_jepa`/`ac_video_jepa`. A team would: write a graph `Dataset` that emits per-patch GNN embeddings `[p, d]` (the GINE + METIS + 1-hop expansion + RWSE front-end is custom and lives outside the eb_jepa core — add it as a new encoder in `eb_jepa/architectures.py` and a new dataset under `eb_jepa/datasets/`), pick one context patch and `m` target patches, and run the image-style trainer over them. The downstream eval (mean-pool patches → frozen linear probe) matches eb_jepa's frozen-feature protocol directly.

**The key contrast with the eb_jepa recipe (the load-bearing hackathon insight).** Graph-JEPA prevents collapse the **I-JEPA/BYOL way: EMA target encoder + stop-gradient + weak predictor, with NO explicit regularizer.** The eb_jepa examples take the *opposite* route per the guide: `image_jepa` is a JEA (no predictor) and collapse is handled by an **explicit VICReg variance-covariance loss or SIGReg/BCS** term (the "two-view SSL loss" contract: `(z1,z2) → dict with "loss"`), with **no EMA target and no stop-grad**. This gives a sharp, self-contained hackathon contribution: build **"EB-Graph-JEPA"** = keep Graph-JEPA's METIS-patch + GNN + context/target masking, but **drop the EMA/stop-grad and regularize patch embeddings with VICReg or SIGReg (`BCS`)** instead. The controlled comparison the guide rewards: faithful EMA Graph-JEPA vs the eb_jepa-native regularized variant, on MUTAG/DD/REDDIT (where the reported numbers are SOTA), three seeds, one figure showing the collapse curve at zero covariance weight — mirroring the image_jepa SIGReg-vs-EMA story and the Laya/LeJEPA "SIGReg is the load-bearing anti-collapse term" thread already in this memory.

**A second, orthogonal angle to test.** Graph-JEPA's distinctive **hyperbolic-angle 2D-coordinate target** is independent of the regularizer choice. A team could ablate it inside eb_jepa: Euclidean latent target + VICReg vs hyperbolic-coord target + VICReg, testing whether the hierarchy trick survives when the anti-collapse mechanism changes (Table 3 only ablated it under the EMA recipe).

**What's realistic in 24 h.** TUD datasets are tiny (MUTAG: 188 graphs avg 17.9 nodes; even REDDIT-M5 is 4,999 graphs) and training is <1 min to ~18 min per run on a single GPU per the paper, so full reproduction *plus* an eb_jepa-regularizer ablation comfortably fits the budget — far cheaper than the audio/video tracks. Per-feature normalization caveat from the guide applies: z-score the GNN patch embeddings before the variance term if going the VICReg route.

## Caveats & open threads
- **High downstream variance** (±3–6 BA on several TUD sets) is intrinsic here, driven by random single-context-patch selection and collapse-proneness with small similar patches; the authors did **not extensively tune hyperparameters** ("our focus was on demonstrating the architecture's efficacy with a simple training regimen"), so numbers are a floor.
- **Fails on localized-interaction tasks**: Peptides-func multilabel classification drops to 0.263 AP (vs 0.644 SAN+RWSE) — the single-context subgraph objective "fails to capture localized, specific interactions." Graph-JEPA suits smooth global graph-level properties, not fine-grained per-node/edge tasks.
- **Graph-level only.** Future work explicitly names "extending the proposed method to node and edge-level learning"; out of the box it produces one vector per graph.
- **Hyperbolic target is a heuristic approximation**, not true hyperbolic geometry — it estimates "a restricted part of hyperbolic space" (a hyperboloid section); the optimal latent geometry for general graph SSL is left open. Poincaré objective is numerically unstable in high dim (NaN on IMDB-B).
- **The GNN/METIS/RWSE front-end is non-trivial to bolt onto eb_jepa** — it is the most involved part of a port (custom encoder + custom dataset dispatch, as the guide's "add a dataset/modality" row warns), so scope it early.
