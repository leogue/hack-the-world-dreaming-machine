# JEPA-WMs — A systematic study of which design choices make latent-space planning with frozen-encoder Joint-Embedding Predictive World Models actually work, yielding a recipe that beats DINO-WM and V-JEPA-2-AC

**Authors:** Basile Terver, Tsung-Yen Yang, Jean Ponce, Adrien Bardes, Yann Le Cun (Meta FAIR / Inria Paris / ENS-PSL / NYU) **Venue/Year:** Transactions on Machine Learning Research (TMLR), 05/2026 (OpenReview id cHZn5Gdh8e) **arXiv:** 2512.24497 (v3, 18 May 2026) **Repo:** https://github.com/facebookresearch/jepa-wms

## TL;DR
The paper names the family of action-conditioned world models that train a predictor purely by a JEPA prediction loss in the embedding space of a (usually frozen) visual encoder, then plan by optimizing actions in that latent space, "JEPA-WMs" (formalized as a unified recipe, not a new algorithm; DINO-WM, V-JEPA-2-AC, PLDM, Navigation World Models, EB-JEPA are instances). It then does a large factor-by-factor ablation of the components that matter, holding the encoder frozen and focusing on learning the *dynamics* (predictor). Studied axes: planning optimizer, planning cost (L1/L2), multistep rollout loss, proprioception, training context length W, encoder type, predictor architecture/conditioning, model/data scaling. Combining the winners gives a "JEPA-WM optimum" that outperforms DINO-WM and V-JEPA-2-AC on navigation (Maze, Wall, Push-T) and manipulation (Metaworld, DROID/Robocasa). The headline practical message: the best recipe is *task-dependent* (small DINOv2-S + depth-6 predictor + proprio + 2-step rollout for simulated nav; DINOv3-L + depth-12 predictor, no proprio, 6-step rollout for real-world manipulation), CEM-L2 planning is the safe default, and scaling model size only helps on real-world data.

## Problem & motivation
World models trained from state-action trajectories and used with a planner are a popular route to general physical agents. A recent family plans in the *learned representation space* of the world model rather than input/pixel space, betting that abstracting away irrelevant detail makes planning more sample-efficient (DINO-WM, V-JEPA-2-AC, PLDM, NWM). But the design space is large and the prior works each fix many choices implicitly, so the literature lacks a controlled answer to: given a pretrained (frozen) visual encoder, *how do you best learn a latent dynamics model for manipulation and navigation planning?* The paper fills that gap with a systematic study of the predictor and planner (encoder kept frozen, as in DINO-WM / V-JEPA-2-AC), and uses the findings to build a model that beats those baselines.

## Method
**Setup (Eqs. 1-4).** Observations are embedded by a frozen visual encoder `E_vis_phi` plus an optional shallow proprioceptive encoder `E_prop_theta`; an action encoder `A_theta` embeds actions; a ViT predictor `P_theta` takes state+action embeddings and predicts the next-state embedding. Only `E_prop`, `A`, `P` are trained; `E_vis` is frozen. Training loss is MSE between predicted and target embeddings (teacher forcing), with a **frame-causal attention mask** so the predictor is simultaneously trained for all context lengths 0..W-1 (W=3 default). Frameskip f packs f actions into one `f x A` action so the predictor predicts the outcome of several actions.

**Planning (Eqs. 2-4).** Given start/goal observations, minimize a planning cost `L_p = (L_vis + alpha * L_prop)` between the predictor's H-step unroll `F_phi,theta` and the goal embedding, over an action sequence in `R^{H x A}`, via closed-loop MPC. Test-time sliding context `W_p` must satisfy `W_p <= W` (training context); they use `W_p = 2` (two frames to infer velocity).

**Studied design choices (each varied independently from a DINO-WM-style base: DINOv2 ViT-S encoder, depth-6 predictor, no proprio).**
- **Planner:** CEM (CMA-ES-style), a new Nevergrad/NGOpt-wizard interface (called NG, zero hyperparameter tuning), Adam, and GD (the cost is differentiable). Cost: L1 vs L2.
- **Multistep rollout loss (Eq. 5):** add k-step rollout terms `L_1 + ... + L_k` with truncated BPTT. Framed as data augmentation against compounding autoregressive error; an accuracy-robustness tradeoff analyzed via the predictor's effective Lipschitz constant `Lambda_K` vs one-step error `delta_K`. Trains up to 6-step (needs W=7).
- **Proprioception:** jointly-trained proprio encoder + proprio loss term (unlike V-JEPA-2-AC which omits the proprio loss) vs vision-only.
- **Training context length W:** 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 14.
- **Encoder type:** DINOv2 (S/B/L), DINOv3-L, V-JEPA-L, V-JEPA-2-L (out-of-the-box weights; local/dense features; frame-pair encoding equalized to same token count).
- **Predictor architecture/conditioning:** feature-conditioning + sincos (DINO-WM style), sequence-conditioning + RoPE (V-JEPA-2 style), feature-cond + RoPE, AdaLN + RoPE, AdaLN-zero + RoPE (AdaLN injects action info at every block).
- **Model & data scaling:** encoder ViT-S/B/L with matched predictor width; predictor depth 3/6/9/12; data fractions 2/10/50/100%.

## Key results
- **Planner:** CEM-L2 is best or competitive everywhere and is fixed as the default. L2 cost beats L1 consistently. Gradient planners (Adam/GD) win on smooth, greedily-reachable cost landscapes (Metaworld, Robocasa) but fail badly on non-greedy 2D navigation (Wall/Push-T/Maze) and on contact-rich DROID (local minima / multi-modal landscapes). The new NG planner matches CEM on real-world manipulation (DROID, Robocasa) with no hyperparameter tuning, but loses to CEM on precise navigation (slower, more exploratory convergence).
- **Multistep rollout:** going from teacher-forcing to 2-step helps; in *simulated* envs accuracy dominates and the optimum is small K=2 (more steps hurt); on *DROID* the Lipschitz-reduction benefit dominates and the optimum shifts to K=6.
- **Proprioception:** consistently helps (resolves the Metaworld "oscillate near the goal" failure where vision alone can't read off small displacements). Not applicable for DROID->Robocasa zero-shot (proprio spaces unaligned).
- **Context length W:** big jump from W=1 to W=2 (velocity); simulated optimum W=3, DROID optimum W=5 (richer real dynamics). Too-long W hurts (fewer unique training slices; e.g. W=14 keeps only 86% of DROID videos) - echoes PLDM's "stitching suboptimal trajectories" point.
- **Encoder:** DINO (v2/v3) clearly beats V-JEPA video encoders - fine object segmentation puts distinct objects on distinct tokens so the predictor learns localized, sparse token dynamics. DINOv3 beats DINOv2 only on photorealistic envs (DROID, Robocasa); on toy 2D envs DINOv2 is enough and DINOv3 can converge slower/lower.
- **Predictor architecture:** AdaLN+RoPE best on average (per-block conditioning avoids action-info vanishing, compute-efficient) but advantage is slight and task-dependent (sincos+ftcond wins on Metaworld). RoPE vs sincos: no substantial difference. AdaLN-zero underperforms AdaLN on the most reliable-signal envs (DROID, Push-T, Maze), so AdaLN is chosen for the final model.
- **Scaling:** model size (encoder+predictor width, predictor depth) does *not* help on simulated envs (saturation / harder planning landscape / underfitting under fixed compute; optimal depth ~6, possibly 3 for the simplest 2D tasks) but *consistently helps on DROID/Robocasa* (real, complex dynamics). Data scaling helps all methods and all datasets; the proposed model's advantage is largest where data is least saturated (DROID, Wall).
- **Final "JEPA-WM optimum" (Table 2)** beats DINO-WM (DWM) and V-JEPA-2-AC (VJ2AC, retrained with a rollout-loss bug fix) on most tasks. Ours vs DWM: Maze 83.9 vs 81.6, Wall 78.8 vs 64.1, Push-T 70.2 vs 66.0, MW-R 58.2 vs 44.8, MW-RW 41.6 vs 35.1, Rc-R 25.4 vs 19.1, Rc-Pl 30.7 vs 21.7, DROID 48.2 vs 39.4 (VJ2AC: Rc-R 16.2, Rc-Pl 33.1, DROID 42.9). Recipes: simulated nav = DINOv2-S encoder + depth-6 ViT-S predictor, AdaLN+RoPE, proprio, 2-step rollout, W=3; real manip = DINOv3-L encoder + depth-12 ViT-L predictor, no proprio, 6-step rollout, W=5; CEM-L2 throughout.
- **Limitation flagged:** the deterministic MSE predictor learns the conditional mean of multi-modal futures; mitigated here by deterministic benchmarks + latent abstraction + closed-loop MPC, but stochastic/aleatoric envs would need latent-variable or latent-diffusion extensions.

## Relevance to the EB-JEPA hackathon
This is the facebookresearch/jepa-wms scale-up codebase for planning with (mostly) frozen encoders that the hackathon guide points to, and the paper is cited in the guide as `terver2026JEPAWMs` (Track 9). The eb_jepa toy recipe in the hackathon is the minimal version of exactly this family: a JEPA world model (VC + temporal-similarity + IDM regularizers) planned with a distance-cost MPPI/CEM planner on Two Rooms. JEPA-WMs is the "what scales and what breaks" companion. Concrete mappings:
- **Track 7 (learned cost / value for planning):** the paper shows the planning *cost* matters a lot (L2 > L1 everywhere; gradient planners die on non-greedy 2D nav because raw latent distance is a poor, locally-minimum-ridden cost) and explicitly calls a better-than-distance cost an open thread. Direct motivation for a TD-MPC-style learned cost/value head in `eb_jepa/planning.py` (`objective_name_map`); validate on Two Rooms' hardest non-monotonic episodes.
- **Track 8 (hierarchical / multi-timescale world model):** the paper's multistep-rollout, context-length, and scaling findings (compounding error, accuracy-robustness Lipschitz tradeoff, long-horizon failure) are the quantitative case for a coarse long-horizon predictor on top of the fine per-step one; map the two-level unroll into a forked `ac_video_jepa/main.py`. The paper's modular encoder/predictor split is the natural starting point.
- **Track 9 (stress-test the recipe under factors of variation):** directly named here - reuse the JEPA-WMs benchmark suite (Maze, Wall, Push-T, Metaworld, DROID, Robocasa) and its frozen-encoder + `JEPA.unroll` + planner stack rather than hand-building an environment, to dial up visual/geometric/physical factors and see which eb_jepa regularizer term (VC vs sim vs IDM) breaks first vs the Two Rooms baseline. The encoder-type and scaling results say: expect DINOv3-L + deeper predictor + more proprio/context to be the terms that must be strengthened as the world gets harder.

The recipe transfers cleanly: keep CEM-L2 as the planning default, add proprioception and a 2-step rollout loss before reaching for bigger encoders, and only scale model size once the environment's dynamics are genuinely complex.

## Caveats / open threads
- Deterministic predictor / MSE = conditional-mean of multi-modal futures; needs stochastic (latent-variable or latent-diffusion) extension for aleatoric environments. Benchmarks here are (near-)deterministic, so this is untested at scale.
- Encoder is frozen throughout (the study is deliberately about the *predictor*); jointly-trained-encoder JEPA-WMs (PLDM, EB-JEPA) are named as in-family but not ablated here - so "DINOv2/3 > V-JEPA" is a statement about frozen out-of-the-box features, not about learned encoders.
- Best recipe is strongly task-dependent (simulated vs real-world); no single config wins everywhere, and several architecture results (AdaLN vs sincos, AdaLN vs AdaLN-zero) are slight and noisy on low-signal envs.
- V-JEPA-2-AC numbers are from a *retrained* model with a rollout-loss bug fix, not the public checkpoint, so the head-to-head is against the authors' reimplementation.
- Real-world "DROID" evaluation is offline action-error on 16 in-lab Franka videos (rescaled to an Action Score), and Robocasa is zero-shot from DROID with a domain gap - not closed-loop on a physical robot.
